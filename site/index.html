Keep your world-model
honest with itself.

You hold a set of natural-language claims. This tool does not parse them — you paste its prompt into whatever LLM you like, the LLM returns a strict SMT-LIB2 formalization, you paste that back. Then a real solver (Z3, compiled to wasm, running entirely in this tab) tells you the smallest set of your claims that can't all be true, and the things you never asserted but are now committed to. The copy-paste round-trip is the seam where natural language meets logic — surfaced on purpose, never hidden. Goal: consistency, not truth.

What this is — and what it is not

The seam is the point. Three places this is honest about, none hidden:
  1. NL → SMT-LIB2 faithfulness is done by you + your LLM, in the open, by copy-paste. The tool guarantees consistency of the formalization, never that the formalization faithfully captures your sentence. That is yours to confirm — cheaply, in the loop.
  2. Z3 can answer unknown on hard quantified fragments. When it does, it says so. It is not silently treated as "consistent".
  3. Registry discipline (reuse a symbol, don't mint a synonym) is enforced by Z3's own type-checker: a bad symbol surfaces as Z3's verbatim error, not a guess.
The goal is consistency, not truth: the tool never claims your claims are right — only whether they can all hold at once, which is the smallest, and the only fully-mechanizable, part of the problem.

Engine: a Rust core (wmt-core, wasm) owns the registry, the trajectory, AGM selection, and SMT assembly — it does not solve. The solver is Z3 4.16 (wasm), running in this tab; the same SMT-LIB2 is verified end-to-end against the native Z3 binary in the crate's test suite. First load fetches ~34 MB of Z3 wasm (then cached).
